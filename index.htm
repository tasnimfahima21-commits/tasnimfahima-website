International trade is the exchange of goods, services, and capital across national borders, and it plays a vital role in shaping the global economy.
allows countries to specialize in the production of goods and services that they can create most efficiently, while importing items that are produced more effectively elsewhere. This system of comparative advantage increases overall productivity, lowers costs for consumers, and provides a wider variety of products in the global market.
For example, a country with fertile land and favorable climate may export agricultural products, while another with advanced technology may export machinery or software.
Beyond the movement of goods, international trade fosters cultural exchange, strengthens diplomatic ties, and encourages innovation by exposing businesses to global competition. However, it also presents challenges such as trade imbalances, protectionist policies, dependency on foreign markets, and the risk of economic disruption from global crises like pandemics or conflicts.
Organizations such as the World Trade Organization (WTO) and regional trade agreements like the European Union or ASEAN play important roles in regulating, facilitating, and promoting fair trade practices.
Overall, international trade remains a key driver of economic growth and globalization, linking nations together through a complex web of interdependence that affects nearly every aspect of modern life.

    But Nonna Rosa had one rule carved in stone: “Pizza is for the hands. Pasta is for the fork. Never mix them!” One summer afternoon, a tourist from Canada walked into the trattoria. His Italian wasn’t great, but his hunger was massive. He looked at the menu, scratched his head, and said: “Can I have… pizza with pasta on top?” The whole restaurant froze. A fork clattered to the floor. Somewhere, an accordion stopped playing. “Pizza… con pasta?!” Nonna Rosa repeated, clutching her heart. But she was also a woman of curiosity. So, with a dramatic sigh, she decided to try. She made a thin, crispy pizza base, spread fresh tomato sauce, sprinkled mozzarella, then piled on steaming tagliatelle tossed with basil pesto. She served it to the Canadian with a shake of her head, expecting disaster.
    The man took a bite… and his eyes lit up. The room waited. “This,” he declared, “is the best thing I’ve ever eaten.” Within a week, locals were secretly ordering pizza con pasta.     In a small mountain town, there was a tea garden café run by an old woman named Aunty Reba. She wasn’t rich, but her cooking had magic in it—especially her pumpkin soup.
    One winter, a fierce snowstorm hit. Roads closed, markets emptied, and people were stuck in their homes without much to eat. But Aunty Reba? She had a habit of saving every pumpkin seed from the autumn harvest, drying them, and planting more the next season. She also stored jars of her special spice mix—ginger, nutmeg, cinnamon, and a secret pinch of something no one could guess.
    When the snow trapped the whole town, she opened her little café and began making giant pots of soup. People trudged through the snow just to have a bowl—hot steam fogging up the windows, the smell of roasted pumpkin wrapping around them like a blanket.
    No one paid in money that week. Instead, they brought whatever they had—apples, tea leaves, firewood, even a rabbit someone had found wandering in the cold (don’t worry, it became a pet, not dinner).
    When spring came, people still remembered that soup—not just for its taste, but because it reminded them that food isn’t only about feeding the body. It’s about warming the soul, bringing people together when the world feels cold.


    Political science is the study of politics, government systems, and the behavior of political actors. It explores how power is gained, used, and contested in societies, as well as how policies are made and their impact on people. Here’s an overview of main areas and subjects in political science:
    Political Theory
    • Philosophical foundations of politics (Plato, Aristotle, Machiavelli, Locke, Marx, etc.)
    • Concepts like liberty, justice, equality, democracy, rights, and the role of the states Comparative Politics
    • Comparing political systems, institutions, and processes across countries.
    • Topics: democracies vs. authoritarian regimes, electoral systems, political parties. International Relations (IR)
    • How states interact globally.
    • Topics: diplomacy, war, peace, international organizations (UN, NATO), globalization. Public Administration & Policy
    • How governments implement decisions.
    • Public sector management, policy-making, and administrative law. Political Economy
    • Relationship between politics and economics.
    • Topics: capitalism, socialism, development, trade, and resource distribution.
    *Additional Specializations
    • Constitutional Law – study of constitutions and legal frameworks.
    • Political Sociology – how society shapes politics and vice versa.
    • Environmental Politics – political solutions to environmental issues.
    • Gender and Politics – role of gender in political representation and policy.
    • Security Studies – national and international security, terrorism, and conflict resolution.
    *Skills You Gain
    • Research and analysis – understanding and interpreting political data.
    • Critical thinking – questioning policies, power structures, and ideologies.
    • Communication – writing persuasive arguments and presenting ideas clearly.
    *Career Paths
    Graduates in political science can work in:
    • Government and civil service
    • International organizations (UN, EU, etc.)
    • NGOs and advocacy groups
    • Journalism and media
    • Academia and research
    • Political consulting and campaign management
